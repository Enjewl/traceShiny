ICARUS Version 3 - 26th October, 2023

    • Release of this version introduces methods for efficient computational analysis of large datasets up to one million cells! The update in question, ICARUS v3 utilises the cell sketching method to subsample cells from the overall dataset in which serves as a basis for dimensionality reduction and clustering that can be then applied to the large dataset. This drastically lowers computational time whilst retaining power to detect heterogeneity across the data.
    • NEW Sketch-based methods to perform dimensionality reduction and clustering.
    • NEW Utilising this population of sketched cells to perform common downstream data analysis including co-expression network analysis, regulatory gene network construction, trajectory analysis, cell-cell signalling and examination of cell cluster association with GWAS traits (all previous implemented functionality in ICARUS).
    • NEW Sketch-based methods adapted to DoubletFinder doublet detection. ICARUS v3 utilises the dataset of sketched cells to perform pANN generation at a small scale which then are projected back to the overall dataset (ProjectData functionality of Seurat v5) to enable approximation of multiplets at a large scale.
    • NEW Cell type annotation against large cell atlases including:
      1. Tabula sapiens: first-draft human cell atlas of nearly 500,000 cells from 24 organs of 15 normal human subjects.
      2. Tabula Muris Senis: A single-cell transcriptomic atlas across the lifespan of Mus musculus that includes data from 23 tissues and organs.
      3. Human Lung Cell Atlas: Single-cell atlas of 49 datasets of the human respiratory system spanning over 2.4 million cells from 486 individuals.
      4. Asian Immune Diversity Atlas (AIDA): 1,058,909 peripheral blood mononuclear cells from 503 healthy donors spanning five Asian ancestries across Japan, Singapore, and South Korea alongside common controls via 5’-single-cell RNA sequencing.
      5. Developing Human Immune system: Single-cell RNA sequencing of cells from yolk sac, prenatal spleen, and skin spanning weeks 4 to 17 after conception.
      6. Human Liver: 73,295 cells from the healthy human liver.
      7. Adult Human Retina: Single-cell atlas of the adult human retina, including more than 250,000 nuclei for single-nuclei RNA-seq.
    • DEPRECATED Multi-modal analysis no longer compatible with large datasets.
    • DEPRECATED ALRA method of imputing dropouts no longer compatible with large datasets.

ICARUS Version 2.0 - 21st April, 2022

    • NEW Perform trajectory analysis with Monocle3, read more about it here.
    • NEW Detection and removal of doublets in data with DoubletFinder, read more about it here.
    • NEW Cell cluster labelling with sctype. The sctype reference dataset compiled cell markers from CellMarker and PanglaoDB databases and includes markers for the Immune system, Pancreas, Liver, Eye, Kidney, Brain, Lung, Adrenal, Heart, Intestine, Muscle, Placenta, Spleen, Stomach and Thymus.
    • NEW Reference datasets for cell labelling with SingleR. These include:
      Brain
      • Darmanis Brain Data: scRNA-seq of 8 adult and 4 fetal human brain temporal lobe tissue (Darmanis et al., 2015).
      • Zhong Prefrontal Cortex Data: scRNA-seq of developing human embryonic prefrontal cortex from gestational weeks 8 to 26 (Zhong et al., 2018).
      Pancreas
      • Baron Pancreas Data: scRNA-seq of pancreatic cells from 4 human donors and 2 mouse strains (Baron et al., 2016).
      • Lawlor Pancreas Data: scRNA-seq of 5 non-diabetic and 8 type 2 diabetic human islet samples (Lawlor et al., 2017).
      • Muraro Pancreas Data: scRNA-seq of pancreatic cells from 4 human donors (Muraro et al., 2016).
      • Segerstolpe Pancreas Data: scRNA-seq of human islet cells from 6 healthy and 4 type 2 diabetic donors (Segerstolpe et al., 2016).
      Multiple organs
      • He Organ Atlas: scRNA-seq of 15 tissue organs of one adult donor. Tissue organs include bladder, blood, common bile duct, oesophagus, heart, liver, lymph node, bone marrow, muscle, rectum, skin, small intestine, spleen, stomach and trachea (He et al., 2020).
